We view AI automation differently. Not as something magical. Not as a gimmick, a hype, or a trick. But as a fundamental way to reorganize our work.

For us, AI automation consists of three layers.

First: automate processes. Work that happens manually today must simply stop costing time.

Then: enrich processes. Not just doing what you already did faster, but making things possible that weren't before.

And only then: unlock your company's full potential. Not by working harder, but by organizing smarter.

Most companies skip this sequence. They start with potential, with vision, with big promises. While the reality is that they're drowning in operational work.

That gap between ambition and daily practice is precisely why so many AI initiatives fail. Not because AI doesn't work, but because people start by talking instead of building.

Why this matters more than ever

Rob and I have seen this pattern not just with clients, but with ourselves. At our previous companies, we were constantly trying to optimize work. Improving processes. Streamlining meetings. Adding structure. That worked sometimes, sometimes not, but it took months.

When AI matured, we saw something shift. Not theoretically, but practically. Work that used to take weeks could be done in days. And what could be done in days can now (sometimes) be done in hours. And with the right setup, even automatically. Not perfect. But reliable. And above all: consistent.

What surprised us wasn't how little AI could do, but how mature the technology is. And at the same time, how little of it was actually being applied. Organizations rarely see the same possibilities as we do. Instead of building, they asked for AI strategies. For long trajectories. For plans for 'organization-wide adoption'. While in the meantime, they were having the same conversations every week, making the same notes, and forgetting the same follow-ups.

The market moves incredibly fast. But thinking moves slowly. Many companies use AI as if it's mainly a new tool. What we saw: AI doesn't force you to use a different tool, but to work in a different way.

Why so many AI projects get stuck

They start too big. "Transform the entire organization." It sounds ambitious, but it paralyzes. Nobody knows where to start. So nobody really starts anywhere.

They keep talking about possibilities. Workshops, demos, inspiration sessions. Interesting, sure. But without something running in practice, it remains abstract.

They build without ownership. Loose pilots. Loose flows. Nobody feels responsible for the outcome. When it stops, nobody really notices.

They lack control. Automation without insight feels unreliable. So it's not used. Or turned off after a few weeks.

What does work (and why it's uncomfortable)

Start with one process. Not the most important process on paper. But the process where the most time leaks away today. Meetings are a perfect example.

Build something that runs within 30 days. No prototype. No demo. Something that takes work off your hands every week. Automatically. Without anyone having to start it.

Measure results and only then scale up. Time saved. Errors prevented. Calm created. If it's not measurable, it doesn't exist.

This is uncomfortable, especially for consultants. Because speed means fewer billable hours. And concrete results are verifiable. You can't hide behind promises or visions.

But that's exactly where the honesty lies.

Why we started building MAP

The Meeting Automation Platform (MAP) didn't emerge as a product idea. It emerged from frustration. From conversations we knew were valuable, but whose value leaked away afterward.

Not because people weren't doing their work. But because follow-up is manual work. And manual work doesn't scale.

The first version was simple. Record. Summarize. Save. It worked. Sometimes. But it felt fragile. No overview. No control. No trust.

The turning point came when we asked ourselves an honest question: if this grows bigger, do we want to have to rebuild it?

That was the moment MAP stopped being automation and became a system.

MAP is not a tool. It's proof of concept.

MAP is not a tool. It's the first tangible proof of how we at Schakel.AI view software, AI, and work.

At its core, MAP is an internal operating system. An operational nervous system that captures, understands, enriches, and forwards what happens in conversations.

Meetings are no longer isolated events here, but:

  • input for processes
  • data for insight
  • fuel for automation

That's a fundamental difference. Conversations are no longer fleeting or dependent on discipline, but structurally valuable.

Autonomous where possible. Transparent where necessary.

Not as philosophy, but as a working system. And that's precisely what makes it scalable.

From capturing to steering

The real value isn't in meeting notes. Those are just the beginning. By structurally capturing conversations, something new emerges: management data.

Patterns become visible. Quality becomes measurable. Differences between teams become explainable.

Together with our AI colleagues, MAP shifts from capturing to understanding, steering, and preparing. Not as a future vision, but as daily practice.

That's what people often call 'agentic AI'. We just call it the logical next step.

Personally, this was a turning point

For Rob and me, MAP wasn't a technical project. It was a choice. We could keep talking about AI. Or we could build.

Building is harder. You have to make choices. You have to measure. You have to take responsibility for what happens.

But it's also more honest. And ultimately more effective.

In five years, this will be normal. Every company will have autonomous processes. The difference will be who starts today.
